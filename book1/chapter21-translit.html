21 Chapter 21
tirai calacalattatu! The Curtains Parted
oree camayattil oruvaṉukkuɭɭee iraɳʈu maṉaŋkaɭ iyaŋka muʈiyumaa? muʈiyum eṉṟu aṉṟaikku vantiyatteevaṉuʈaiya aṉupavattiliruntu teriya vantatu. Can two minds function at the same time in one body? Yes -- Vandiya Devan knew the answer because of his experiences that day!
cooɻa vaɭa naaʈʈiṟkuɭɭeeyee vaɭam mikunta pirateecattiṉ vaɻiyaaka avaṉ pooyk koɳʈiruntaaṉ. natikaɭil putuppuṉal poŋkip perukik koɳʈirunta kaalam. kaɳavaaykaɭ, matakukaɭ maʈaikaɭiṉ vaɻiyaaka vaaykkaalkaɭilum vayalkaɭilum kupukupuveṉṟu jalam He was travelling through the most fertile of the bountiful Chozla lands. It was the season of new floods in all the rivers and streams. Fresh water rushed through canals, conduits and waterways into the lush fields; water was everywhere.
paayntukoɳʈiruntatu. eŋkee paarttaalum taɳɳiir mayamaayiruntatu. cooɻa teecattai 'vaɭanaaʈu' eṉṟum cooɻa maṉṉaṉai 'vaɭavaṉ' eṉṟum kuuṟuvatu evvaɭavu poruttamaaṉatu? - ippaʈi eɳɳiyavuʈaṉee cooɻa naaʈʈukkum cooɻa maṉṉaṉukkum eeṟpaʈʈirunta apaayaŋkaɭ avaṉ niṉaivukku vantaṉa. inta nilaimaiyil taṉṉuʈaiya kaʈamai eṉṉa? iɭavaracar karikaalar koʈutta olaiyai maʈʈum cakkaravarttiyiʈam ceerppittu viʈʈut taṉ kaʈamai tiirntatu eṉṟu iruntuviʈuvataa? inta iraajakulat taayaatik kaayccalilum puucalilum naam etaṟkaakat talaiyiʈʈukkoɭɭa veeɳʈum? cooɻa naaʈʈuc cimmaacaṉattukku yaar vantaal taaṉ namakku eṉṉa? paarkkap pooṉaal, nammuʈaiya kulattiṉ puurviikap pakaivarkaɭ taaṉee ivarkaɭ? cooɻarkaɭum kaŋkarkaɭum vaitumparkaɭum ceerntu koɳʈutaaṉee vaaɳakooppaaʈi raajyamee illaatapaʈi ceytu viʈʈaarkaɭ? iṉṟaikku aatitta karikaalar nammiʈam aṉpaaka iruntatiṉaal anta aniitiyellaam maṟaintu pooyviʈumaa?... How appropriate, to call the Chozla land bounteous and its monarch the king of bounty! Thoughts of the dangers surrounding the Chozla monarch immediately followed. What is my duty in this situation? Shall I keep quiet after delivering the letters sent by Prince Karikala to the Emperor and think that my duty is done? Why should I interfere in this discord and hostility between royal cousins? How does it concern me, whoever sits on the Chozla throne? In some ways, if I think of it, these Chozlas are my ancestral enemies. Did not the Chozlas, Gangas and Vaithumbas join together to destroy the very existence of my forefathers of the Vanakapadi Kingdom? Can I forget all that injustice just because Prince Karikala befriends me today?
ceeccee! antap paɻaiya campavaŋkaɭai aniitiyeṉṟutaaṉ eppaʈic colla muʈiyum? aracarkaɭ eṉṟaal, oruvarukkoruvar caɳʈaiyiʈuvatu iyaṟkai. atu poolavee veṟṟiyum toolviyum maaṟi maaṟi varuvatum iyaṟkai. veṉṟavarkaɭ miitu tooṟṟavarkaɭ koopaŋkoɭvatil eṉṉa payaṉ? nammuʈaiya muutaataikaɭ nalla nilaimaiyil irunta pootu avarkaɭum maṟṟa aracarkaɭaik katikalaŋkattaaṉee aʈittaarkaɭ? aʈiyooʈu aɻittu viʈattaaṉee paarttaarkaɭ? aa! atu eṉṉa paaʈal? itoo ɲaapakam vantuviʈʈatu! No, no! -- How can I consider that old history as injustice? It is natural that kings fight and oppose each other. Victory and defeat are both natural. What is the use of the defeated group resenting the victorious? When they were powerful, weren't my forefathers ruthless towards the kings they vanquished? Did they not try to completely destroy their enemies? Ah! What was that poem ? I can remember a poem about such activities of my clan:-
"ceeṉai taɻaiyaakkic ceŋkuruti niir teekki
aaṉai mititta aruɲceeṟṟil - maaṉaparaṉ
paaveentar tamveentaṉ vaaɳaṉ paṟittu naʈʈaaṉ
muuveentar taŋkaɭ muʈi!"
With countless armies made into leaf-mulch,
With the flowing red of battlefields stored for irrigation,
In a dirt field ploughed by the war elephant,
That Vaana, the best of kings on this earth,
He planted the heads of the three kings: his enemies.
ippaʈiyellaam poorkkaɭattil koʈuuramaaṉa kaariyaŋkaɭai nam muṉṉoorkaɭum ceytirukkiṟaarkaɭ. poorkkaɭattil tooṟṟavarkaɭiṉ kati eppootum atoo katitaaṉ. iraamaraip poolavum tarma puttiraraip poolavum ellaa aracarkaɭum karuɳai vaɭɭalkaɭaaka iruntuviʈa muʈiyumaa? appaʈi avarkaɭ iruntapaʈiyiṉaal taaṉ kaaʈʈukkup pooyt tiɳʈaaʈiṉaarkaɭ! viira puruʂarkaɭaayiruntum, viirarkaɭiṉ tuɳaiyiruntum vekuvaakak kaʂʈappaʈʈaarkaɭ. iraajariikattil karuɳai eṉpatee kuuʈaatu. paarkkap pooṉaal cooɻa kulattavarkaɭ ciṟitu karuɳaiyuɭɭavarkaɭ eṉṟee collaveeɳʈum. etirikaɭaiyum muʈiyumaaṉaal naɳparkaɭaakkik koɭɭavee paarkkiṟaarkaɭ. ataṟkaakak kulam viʈʈuk kulam kaliyaaɳa campantamum ceytu koɭkiṟaarkaɭ! cuntara cooɻariṉ tantai ariɲcaya cooɻar vaitumparaayaṉ makaɭait tirumaɳam ceytukoɭɭavillaiyaa? aɻakukkup peyarpooṉa antak kaliyaaɳiyiṉ makaṉaayiruppatiṉaal taaṉee cuntara cooɻarum avaruʈaiya makkaɭum kuuʈa cauntariyattil ciṟantu viɭaŋkukiṟaarkaɭ... aa! aɻaki eṉṟatum antak kuʈantai nakarattu maŋkai... aricilaaṟṟaŋkaraip peɳmaɳiyiṉ niṉaivu varukiṟatu. niṉaivu putitaaka eŋkiruntoo vantu viʈavillai. avaṉuʈaiya uɭɭattukkuɭɭeeyee kaṉintu koɳʈirunta niṉaivutaaṉ. My ancestors also committed such dreadful deeds in the battlefield! The lot of those defeated in battle is lamentable. Can all kings be merciful like Rama and Dharma of the epics? Because of their merciful nature they were driven to the forests to suffer! Those epic heros suffered even though they were brave and were befriended by gallant men. There should be no mercy shown in matters of politics! If one thinks about it, we should consider these Chozlas as being more tolerant and merciful than any other such monarchs. If possible, they make friends with their enemies. Didn't King Arinjaya marry the Vaithumba Princess? Isn't it because of that famous beauty, the Princess Kalyani, that Sundara Chozla and his children are so handsome? Ah! ... when I think of beauty, the face of that maiden of Kudanthai, that lady on the banks of River Arisil, comes to my mind! She is not coming into my mind suddenly from somewhere ... her presence has been lurking in my heart all this while...
vantiyatteevaṉuʈaiya veɭimaṉam cooɻa naaʈʈiṉ iyaṟkai vaɭaŋkaɭaip paṟṟiyum iraajariikak kuɻappaŋkaɭaip paṟṟiyum eɳɳik koɳʈirukkaiyil avaṉuʈaiya uɭ maṉam anta maŋkaiyiṉiʈattileeyee iiʈupaʈʈiruntatu. ippootu uɭ maṉam, veɭi maṉam iraɳʈum ottu ammaŋkaiyaik kuṟittup paʈʈavarttaṉamaakac cintikkat toʈaŋkiṉa. piṟaku, veɭiyil enta aɻakaaṉa iyaṟkaip poruɭaip paarttaalum anta maŋkaiyiṉ avayaŋkaɭuʈaṉ oppiʈat tooṉṟiṉa. vaɻuvaɻuppaaṉa muuŋkilaip paarttatum avaɭuʈaiya tooɭkaɭ niṉaivu vantaṉa. oʈaikaɭil maɳʈikkiʈanta kuvaɭai malarkaɭ avaɭuʈaiya kaɳkaɭukku uvamaiyaayiṉa. paŋkaja malarkaɭ avaɭuʈaiya taŋka mukattukku iɳaitaaṉaa eṉṟa aiyam tooṉṟiyatu. natiyoora maraŋkaɭil kuluŋkik koɳʈirunta malarkaɭil vaɳʈukaɭ ceyta riiŋkaarattai avaɭ kuraliṉ olikku uvamai colvatu cariyaakumaa? While his outer mind considered the complications of Chozla politics and enjoyed the beauties of Chozla land, his inner mind dwelt upon that maiden. Now the inner mind and outer mind began to consider the same object, frankly. He began to compare any beautiful sight to her enchanting personality. He recalled her lovely shoulders when he saw the grace of slender bamboos swaying in the breeze. The profuse blooms of creek-side water lilies were example of her dark eyes. He doubted if the lovely lotus could equal her golden face. Can he liken the sweet melody of bees humming in flower groves to her pleasing voice?
ippaʈiyellaam kavikaɭ kaṟpittirukkiṟaarkaɭee tavira, uɳmaiyil ivaiyellaam eŋkee? anta maŋkaiyiṉ cauntariyam eŋkee? avaɭuʈaiya tirumukattaip paarttapootu mey cilirttatee! ippootu niṉaittup paarkkumpootu kuuʈa neɲcu vimmukiṟatee! intap puukkaɭaiyum vaɳʈukaɭaiyum paarttaal attakaiya mey cilirppu uɳʈaakavillaiyee?...ceeccee! mutiyoorkaɭ namakkuc ceyta upateecattaiyellaam maṟantu viʈʈoo m! peɳkaɭiṉ mookattaip pool ulaka vaaɻkkaiyil pollaata maayai veeṟoṉṟumillai. Poets can imagine such nonsense -- how can they be reality? How beautiful she was! The very memory of her face makes my heart beat faster! I am not enthralled like that by these flowers and bees. Oh! Oh! I have forgotten all the advice given by my elders! There is nothing more impermanent in this world than the allure of women. Those who wish to succeed in life should never be enticed by the charm of women.
vaaɻkkaiyil veṟṟi peṟa virumpuvooṉ peɳkaɭiṉ mooka valaiyil viɻavee kuuʈaatu; viɻuntaal avaṉ oɻintaaṉ! koovalaṉ kataitaaṉ anta viʂayattai apuurvamaay eʈuttuc colkiṟatee! koovalaṉ maʈʈum eṉṉa? inta naaɭil viiraati viirarum cooɻa naaʈʈilee iɳaiyaṟṟa celvaakku uɭɭavarumaaṉa periya paɻuveeʈʈaraiyaraip paṟṟi makkaɭ parikaacam peecuŋkaaraɳamum atutaaṉee? aaṉaal makkaɭ uɳmai aṟiyaatavarkaɭ. muuʈu pallakkilee vaittup paɻuveeʈʈaraiyar yaaraik koɳʈu varukiṟaar eṉṟu makkaɭukkut teriyaatu! aakaiyaal muuʈattaṉamaakap peecukiṟaarkaɭ. aaṉaalum, anta maturaantakat teevar tammai avvaɭavu keevalappaʈuttik koɭɭa veeɳʈiyatillai. ciiccii! muuʈupallakkil uʈkaarntu koɳʈu, paɻuveeʈʈaraiyariṉ raaɳiyiṉ staaṉattil maṟaintu koɳʈu uur uuraayp poovataa? itutaaṉ aaɳmaikku aɻakaa? ippaʈiyaavatu iraajyam campaatikka veeɳʈumaa? ippaʈic campaatitta iraajyattaittaaṉ avaraal kaappaaṟṟik koɭɭa muʈiyumaa? paɻuveeʈʈaraiyar mutaliyoorai nampi avarkaɭukku uʈpaʈʈuttaaṉee iraajya paripaalaṉam ceyya veeɳʈum? inta viʂayattil cuntara cooɻa cakkaravartti ceytu varuvatee avvaɭavu cilaakkiyamillaitaaṉ! paɻuveeʈʈaraiyar pooṉṟavarkaɭukku ivvaɭavu atikaaramum celvaakkum avar aɭittirukkak kuuʈaatu. atilum maɳi maɳiyaaka iraɳʈu arumaip putalvarkaɭ irukkum pootu? naaʈellaam aticayikkum aṟivum tiṟaṉum uʈaiya putalvi orutti irukkum pootu...? If one falls prey to their enchantment it is the end! The story of Kovalan is a good example of that -- Why think of Kovalan? Look at Lord Pazluvoor! That most powerful personage, the bravest of soldiers -- aren't people making fun of him because of such a predicament? -- but the people do not know the truth! No one knows about who travels in the closed palanquin with Lord Pazluvoor. They talk in ignorance. Still, Prince Madurandaka need not demean himself in that fashion. Why should he hide in a closed palanquin under the guise of Lord Pazluvoor's queen, and go from town to town? Is this commendable in any man? Should he acquire his kingdom in this manner? Can he safeguard his gains obtained in this fashion? He will have to trust Lord Pazluvoor and his cronies and rule dominated by their power. On that issue -- what Sundara Chozla does is also not commendable; he should not have given such prerogatives and powers to persons like the Lords of Pazluvoor. That too when he has two such capable, exquisite sons. And when he has a daughter whose intelligence and skill is praised by the whole nation...
anta maŋkai, cootiʈar viiʈʈil paarttavaɭ, aaṟṟaŋkaraiyil peeciyavaɭ, - avaɭ mukam yaaruʈaiya jaaʈaiyaayirukkiṟatu?... appaʈiyum irukkalaamoo? - paittiyakkaarattaṉam! oru naaɭum irukka muʈiyaatu! - eeṉ irukka muʈiyaatu? oru veeɭai avvitam iruntaal, nammaip pooṉṟa aṟiviiṉaṉ veeṟu yaarum illai! nammaip pooṉṟa turatirʂʈacaaliyum illai! ilaŋkai mutal vintiya parvatam varaiyil entap peɳɳaraciyiṉ pukaɻ parantu virintu paraviyirukkiṟatoo, avaɭiʈam naam eppeerppaʈʈa kaaʈʈumiraaɳʈiyaip pool naʈantu koɳʈoo m! - naaɭaikku avaɭiʈam eppaʈi iɭavaracariṉ olaiyuʈaṉee ceṉṟu mukattaik kaaʈʈa muʈiyum? That maiden -- whom I saw at the astrologer's house and met on the banks of the river -- whom does she resemble? ... Could it be possible? -- absolute foolishness! It can never be so! But, why not? If it is so, there is none more ill-fated than me. How did I behave?-- so uncivilized -- towards a lady admired by one and all from the Vindhya Mountains to Lanka Island. It is not possible... How can I face her tomorrow when I deliver the Prince's letters?
ippaʈiyaaka eṉṉavellaamoo vaaṉattaiyum puumiyaiyum ceerttu eɳɳamiʈʈukkoɳʈu vantiyatteevaṉ kaaveerik karaiyooʈu vantu tiruvaiyaaṟṟai aʈaintaaṉ. anta uuriṉ vaɭamum aɻakum avaṉ uɭɭattaik koɭɭaikoɳʈaṉa. atu tiruvaiyaaṟutaaṉaa eṉṟu keeʈʈut terintukoɳʈaaṉ. anta aṟputa kʂeettirattiṉ makimaiyaip paṟṟi avaṉ keeɭvippaʈʈiruntatellaam uɳmaikkuk koɲcam kuṟaivaakavee tooṉṟiyatu. With such disconnected thoughts crowding his mind, Vandiya Devan came along the Cauvery to reach the town of Thiru-vai-aru. He stood on the southern banks of the Cauvery, looking across the river to see the town on the northern bank. The fertility and beauty of that country overpowered his heart. He asked a passerby and confirmed that it was indeed Thiru-vai-aru. All that he had heard about that place seemed to be paltry compared to reality.
ɲaaṉacampantar teevaarattil uɭɭa varɳaṉai iŋkee appaʈiyee tatruupamaayk kaaɳkiṟatu. munnuuṟu aaɳʈu kaalattil maaṟutal oṉṟumeeyillai. The description of that town by Gnana-sambanda in his Thevaram poems could be recognized in the actuality. There was no change in three hundred years.
atoo kaaveeriyiṉ karaiyil uɭɭa maraŋkaɭ eṉṉa ceɻippaay vaɭarntirukkiṉṟaṉa! palaa maraŋkaɭil evvaɭavu periya periya palaak kaaykaɭ toŋkukiṉṟaṉa. inta maatiri toɳʈai maɳʈalattil eŋkum paarkkavee muʈiyaatutaaṉ! aakaa! vaɭamaaṉa iʈaŋkaɭukkeṉṟu kuraŋkukaɭ eŋkiruntoo vantu viʈukiṉṟaṉa. avai kiɭaikkuk kiɭai taavuvatu evvaɭavu aɻakaayirukkiṟatu? cammantap perumaaṉ eṉṉa colliyirukkiṟaar? itoo ɲaapakam varukiṟatu! Look at those luscious groves of trees on the Cauvery bank. How big are the fruits hanging from the trunk and branches of the jack-tree! There is nothing like this in the dry Thondai region! Look at those monkeys gathered in these fertile lands. How delightful to see them jump from tree to tree! I remember the descriptions in Gnana-sambanda's poems:
tiruvaiyaaṟṟu viiti muṉai araŋkaŋkaɭil peɳkaɭ naʈaṉam aaʈukiṟaarkaɭ. anta aaʈalukkeeṟṟa paaʈalooʈu mattaɭac cattamum muɻaŋkukiṟatu. anta muɻakkattaik keeʈʈa kuraŋkukaɭ meekaŋkaɭiṉ karjaṉai eṉṟu eɳɳi uyarnta maraŋkaɭiṉ uccaaɳik kiɭaikaɭil eeṟi maɻai varumaa eṉṟu vaaṉattaip paarkkiṉṟaṉa! Maidens dance on the stages set in the street-corners of Thiru-vai-aru. Song and music accompany that dance with melodious drumbeats; Monkeys hearing those drums (mattalam) think that the skies are thundering with an approaching storm: they climb to the top of palm trees and look up at the skies waiting for the rains!
aʈaʈaa! iṉṟaikkum evvaɭavu poruttamaayirukkiṟatu! uyarnta maraŋkaɭil uccaaɳik kiɭaikaɭil kuraŋkukaɭ eeṟukiṉṟaṉa! atu maʈʈumaa? aaʈal paaʈalkaɭukkuriya iṉiya cattaŋkaɭum uurukkuɭɭiruntu varukiṉṟaṉa. yaaɻ, kuɻal, muɻavu, taɳɳumai mutaliya karuvikaɭiṉ oliyuʈaṉ cataŋkaic cattamum ceerntu olikkiṉṟaṉa. iŋkee aaʈukiṟavarkaɭ kaʈampuur campuvaraiyar maaɭikaiyil aaʈiyavarkaɭaip pool kuravaik kuuttarkaɭ alla. aakaa! iŋkee keeʈpatu paɳpaʈʈa iṉiya kaaṉam. kalaic ciṟappu vaaynta parata naaʈʈiyam aaʈuvooriṉ cataŋkai oli. atoo, aaʈʈivaikkum naʈaṉa aaciriyarkaɭ kaiyil piʈitta kooliṉ cattam kuuʈac ceerntu varukiṟatee! How appropriate that description is even now? Look at those long-tailed monkeys scampering among tree-tops. I can hear the sounds of music and dance from the town! Along with the melody of the veena, flute and other such instruments, I can hear the sounds of dancing feet and ankle bells! The dancers here are not frenzied like the gypsy dancers of Kadamboor. This is classical dance -- decorous Bharata Natyam in full dignity. The music is from established classical culture. I can even hear the tap-tap of dance teachers beating time as their students practice!
"koolooʈak koolvaɭaiyaar kuuttaaʈak
kuvimukaiyaar mukattiṉiṉṟu
ceelooʈac cilai yaaʈac ceeyiɻai
yaar naʈamaaʈun tiruvaiyaaṟee!"
Teachers keep time, while beguiling damsels dance;
Poets speak enchanting verse, making every sculpted stone come alive;
Such accomplished people stroll in the streets of Thiru-vai-aru.
aakaa! campanta svaamikaɭ ciṟanta civapaktar; atai kaaʈʈilum ciṟanta rasikar! avar aṉṟaikku varɳaṉai ceytapaʈiyee iṉṟaikkum intat tiruvaiyaaṟu viɭaŋkukiṟatee! ippaʈippaʈʈa uuril orunaaɭ taŋki aaʈal paaʈal vinootaŋkaɭaip paarttuviʈʈu, aiyaaṟapparaiyum aṟam vaɭartta naayaki ammaṉaiyum taricittu viʈʈuttaaṉ pookaveeɳʈum! aʈaaʈaa, kaaveeriyiṉ karaiyil ettaṉai paktarkaɭ uʈkaarntu aṉuʂʈaaṉam ceykiṟaarkaɭ? paʈʈai paʈʈaiyaaka avarkaɭ tiruniiṟu aɳintiruppatu evvaɭavu kaɭaiyaayirukkiṟatu? cila camayam aaʈal paaʈal olikaɭai amukkikkoɳʈu, 'namaccivaaya' mantirattiṉ oli keeʈkiṟatee! atoo campantariṉ teevaarattaiyee yaaroo iṉiya kuralil arumaiyaakap paaʈukiṟaarkaɭee? icaikkum kalaikkum eṉṟee iṟaivaṉ paɳitta uur intat tiruvaiyaaṟu poolum! inta uuril kaʈʈaayam oru naaɭ taŋkip paarttu viʈʈuttaaṉ pookaveeɳʈum! taɲcaavuurukku avacaramaakap pooyttaaṉ eṉṉa payaṉ? kooʈʈaikkuɭ piraveecikka muʈikiṟatoo eṉṉamoo? appaʈip piraveecittaalum makaaraajaaviṉ peeʈʈi kiʈaikkumaa? makaaraajaavaittaaṉ iraɳʈu paɻuveeʈʈaraiyarkaɭumaakac ceerntu ciṟaiyil vaittiruppatu pool vaittirukkiṟaarkaɭaamee...? kaaveeriyiṉ vaʈakaraikkup pooka veeɳʈiyatu taaṉ! Aha! Gnana-sambanda was a good devotee of Shiva but, more than that he was an connoisseur of the arts! This town is exactly as he described it! I must stay in this town at least for tonight and enjoy this music and dance and I must worship at the shrines of the Lord of the five-rivers and the Lady who nurtured justice. Look at all those devotees at prayers on the banks of the Cauvery. How elegant they look with their foreheads adorned with broad ashen marks! Their chants of "Namasivaya" drown the sounds of the music and dance. Look, someone is singing the Thevaram songs so exquisitely. This town seems to be created by the Gods just for music and song! I must stay here tonight. What is the use of hurrying to Tanjore? I don't know if I can even enter the fort? Even if I manage to go in, will I be able to meet the Emperor? The two Lords of Pazluvoor are said to guard the Emperor as if in prison ... Let me cross the Cauvery and go north.
inta muʈivukku vantiyatteevaṉ vantuviʈʈa taruɳattil oru campavam naʈantatu. meeṟkut ticaiyiliruntu kaaveerik karaiyooʈu oru pallakku vantatu. pallakkukku muṉṉaalum piṉṉaalum cila kaaval viirarkaɭum vantaarkaɭ. vantiyatteevaṉukku eetoo oru canteekam tooṉṟiyatu. pallakku arukil varukiṟa varaiyil aŋkeeyee niṉṟu kaattuk koɳʈiruntaaṉ. avaṉ niṉaittapaʈiyee iruntatu. pallakkai muuʈiyirunta veɭit tiraiyil paṉaimarattiṉ ilacciṉaic cittiram kaaɳappaʈʈatu! When Vandiya Devan came to this conclusion, something happened. A palanquin was approaching along the Cauvery from the west. Footmen and guards walked in front and behind the palanquin. A suspicion crossed Vandiya Devan's mind. He waited at the same spot till the palanquin came nearer. His surmise was correct. He could see the sign of the palmtree emblazoned upon the outer screens of the covered palanquin.
aahaa! kaʈampuuriliruntu varukiṟa pallakkuttaaṉ itu! naam kuʈantai vaɻiyaaka vara, ivarkaɭ veeṟoru vaɻiyil vantirukkiṟaarkaɭ! aaṉaal paɻuveeʈʈaraiyaraik kaaɳoom! avar veeṟu eŋkeeyaavatu vaɻiyil taŋkiviʈʈaar poolum. Yes! This palanquin must be coming from Kadamboor! They must have come by some other route, while I came through Kudanthai. But, there is no sign of Lord Pazluvoor! Maybe he is delayed somewhere on the way.
pallakku taɲcaavuur irunta teṉ ticai nookkit tirumpiyatu. avvaɭavutaaṉ, vantiyatteevaṉ tiruvaiyaaṟṟil taŋkum eɳɳattai viʈʈu viʈʈaaṉ. antap pallakkaip piṉ toʈarntu cellat tiirmaaṉittaaṉ. eṉṉa nookkattuʈaṉ appaʈit tiirmaaṉittaaṉ eṉṟaal, atu accamayam avaṉukkee terintirukkavillai. pallakkil viiṟṟiruppatu maturaantakat teevar eṉṟu maʈʈum avaṉukku niccayamaayt terintatu. avar meel eeṟpaʈʈirunta aruvaruppu meelum ciṟitu vaɭarntatu. aaṉaalum pallakkaip piṉ toʈarntu koɲcam pooṉaal, eetaavatu oru nalla cantarppam eeṟpaʈalaam. pallakkaic cumappavarkaɭ ataik kiiɻee vaikkalaam. eeteeṉum oru kaaraɳattukkaaka iɭavaracar maturaantakar veɭippaʈʈu varalaam. accamayam avaruʈaṉ paɻakkam ceytu koɭɭalaam. atu taɲcaavuurk kooʈʈaikkuɭ piraveecikkavum, cakkaravarttiyaip paarkkavum payaṉpaʈalaam. The palanquin turned south on to the Tanjore road. That decided it -- Vandiya Devan abandoned the idea of tarrying at Thiru-vai-aru. He decided to follow that palanquin. Why did he decide to do so? Even he did not know at that time! He was sure that Prince Madurandaka was seated inside the palanquin. His feelings of disgust about the Prince increased. Still, he might find some good opportunity if he followed the palanquin. The bearers might put down the palanquin or the Prince might emerge for some reason or other. He could make his acquaintance and it might help him in entering the fort at Tanjore and meeting the Emperor.
ataṟkut takuntapaʈi eetaavatu koɲcam peeci veeʂam pooʈʈaal pookiṟatu. tantira mantiraŋkaɭaik kaiyaaɭaaviʈʈaal eʈutta kaariyam kaikuuʈaatu allavaa? atilum iraajaaŋkak kaariyaŋkaɭil? I must speak and put up a pretense suitable to achieve my goal. If I don't use such strategy and conniving I cannot complete my assigned task; particularly in such political matters.
eṉavee, pallakkaiyum parivaaraŋkaɭaiyum muṉṉaal pooka viʈʈuc caṟṟup piṉṉaaleeyee vantiyatteevaṉ pooyk koɳʈiruntaaṉ. aaṉaal avaṉ etirpaartta cantarppam oṉṟum kiʈʈavillai. kaaveerikkum taɲcaavuurukkum mattiyilirunta maṟṟum naalu natikaɭaik kaʈantaaki viʈʈatu. appaʈiyum pallakku kiiɻee vaikkappaʈavillai; oree muuccaakap pooykkoɳʈiruntatu. atoo caṟṟut tuurattil taɲcaavuurk kooʈʈai matilum vaacalum teriyat toʈaŋkiviʈʈaṉa. kooʈʈaikkuɭ pallakkup pooyviʈʈaal, appuṟam avaṉ eɳɳam kaikuuʈap poovatillai. ataṟkuɭ tairiyamaakavum tuɳiccalaakavum eeteeṉum oṉṟu ceytaaka veeɳʈum. Therefore, he let the palanquin and its guards go ahead and then followed them at a little distance. But no expected opportunity arose. They had already crossed the remaining four rivers between Thiru-vai-aru and Tanjore. The palanquin was not put down. The bearers walked steadily. He could soon see the ramparts and gates of the outer fort of Tanjore in the distance. Once the palanquin enters the fort, nothing can be done. He must do something bold and fearless.
eṉṉataaṉ vantuviʈum? talaiyaa pooyviʈum? appaʈip pooṉaal taaṉ pookiṟatee? - eʈutta kaariyattai muʈikkaamal uyirooʈu tirumpip poovatil eṉṉa laapam? itaṟkellaam aʈippaʈaiyil maturaantakatteevar peeril vantiyatteevaṉukku koopam veeṟu iruntatu. pallakkiṉ muuʈutiraiyaik kiɻitteṟintu uɭɭeeyiruppatu peɳɳalla, miicai muɭaitta aaɳpiɭɭai eṉpatai veɭippaʈuttaveeɳʈum eṉṟu avaṉ kai uuṟiyatu; avaṉ uɭɭam tuʈituʈittatu. What can happen? Will they chop off my head? It's alright if they do so -- there is no point going back with my life if I am unable to complete my assigned task. As a basis for all this thought, Vandiya Devan had a certain anger and disgust about the prince in the palanquin. His hands itched to tear apart the curtains of the palanquin and expose the fact that the person inside was not a woman but a full grown man! His heart was agitated!
itaṟku eṉṉa vaɻi eṉṟu avaṉ tiiviramaaka yoocittuk koɳʈirukkaiyil pallakkooʈu ceṉṟa parivaaraŋkaɭil oruvaṉ, caṟṟup piṉ taŋki vantiyatteevaṉai uṟṟu nookkiṉaaṉ. While he was considering various options to execute his intentions, one of the men in the retinue of the palanquin hesitated. He stared at Vandiya Devan seated on his horse.
"nii yaar appaa! tiruvaiyaaṟṟiliruntu eŋkaɭai eeṉ toʈarntu varukiṟaay?" eṉṟu keeʈʈaaṉ. "Who are you Sir? You have been following us from Thiru-vai-aru?" he asked.
"naaṉ uŋkaɭait toʈarntu varavillai aiyaa! taɲcaavuurukkup pookiṟeeṉ! intac caalaitaaṉee taɲcaavuur pookiṟatu!" eṉṟaaṉ vantiyatteevaṉ. "I am not following you Sir! I am going to Tanjore. Doesn't this road lead to Tanjore?"
"inta caalai taɲcaavuurukkuttaaṉ pookiṟatu. aaṉaal itil mukkiyamaaṉavarkaɭ maʈʈumee pookalaam; maṟṟavarkaɭukku veeṟu caalai irukkiṟatu!" eṉṟaaṉ viiraṉ. "Yes. This is the road to Tanjore; but, only important persons can use this road. There is another road for commoners," said that man.
"appaʈiyaa? aaṉaal naaṉum rompa rompa mukkiyamaaṉa maṉuʂaṉ taaṉ!" eṉṟaaṉ vantiyatteevaṉ. "Is that so? I too am quite an important person!" said Vallavarayan Vandiya Devan.
ataik keeʈʈa avviiraṉ puṉṉakai ceytuviʈʈu, "taɲcaikku etaṟkaakap pookiṟaay?" eṉṟaaṉ. The man smiled upon hearing this, "Why are you going to Tanjore?" he asked.
"eṉ cittappaa taɲcaiyil irukkiṟaar. avarukku nooy eṉṟaṟintu paarkkap pookiṟeeṉ" eṉṟu kuuṟiṉaaṉ vantiyatteevaṉ. "My uncle lives in Tanjore. I heard that he was not keeping good health and am going to visit him."
"uṉ cittappaa taɲcaiyil eṉṉa ceykiṟaar? araɳmaṉaiyil uttiyookam paarkkiṟaaraa?" "What does you uncle do in Tanjore? Is he in service at the Palace?"
"illai, illai; cattirattil maɳiyakkaararaayirukkiṟaar" "Oh, no! He is an inn-keeper."
"okoo! appaʈiyaa! cari, eŋkaɭukku muṉṉaal nii poovatutaaṉee? eeṉ piṉṉaaleeyee vantukoɳʈirukkiṟaay?" "Ah! Is that so! Then, why don't you go before us? Why do you follow us?"
"kutirai kaɭaittup pooyirukkiṟatu aiyaa! ataṉaaltaaṉ! illaaviʈil uŋkaɭ mutukaip paarttuk koɳʈee varuvatil eṉakku eṉṉa tirupti?" "My horse is tired Sir. That's why. Otherwise what pleasure do I have, to see your backsides?"
ippaʈip peecikkoɳʈee vantiyatteevaṉ pallakkiṉ arukil vantuviʈʈaaṉ. uʈaṉee avaṉ muuɭaiyai viraʈʈik kaɳʈupiʈikka muyaṉṟa upaayamum pulappaʈʈuviʈʈatu. kutiraiyaik kaalkaɭaal amukki, mukakkayiṟṟai iɻuttu, pallakkiṉ piṉ taɳʈait tuukkiyavarkaɭiṉ peeril viʈʈittaaṉ. avarkaɭ payattuʈaṉ tirumpip paarttaarkaɭ. In the course of this conversation, Vandiya Devan came very close to the palanquin. An idea had occurred to him. He pulled in the reins, pressed both his legs against its flanks and pushed his horse towards the bearers carrying the end poles of the palanquin. They turned in fear and surprise.
vantiyatteevaṉ uʈaṉee, "mahaaraajaa! mahaaraajaa! pallakkut tuukkum aaɭkaɭ eṉ kutiraiyai iʈikkiṟaarkaɭ! aiyoo!" eṉṟu kattiṉaaṉ. Vandiya Devan immediately started shouting "Oh King, My Lord! My Sire! Your bearers are pushing against my horse! Help! Help! Oh dear!"
pallakkai muuʈiyirunta tirai calacalattatu. The screens shook and parted.
