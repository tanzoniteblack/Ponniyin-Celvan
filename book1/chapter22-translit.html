22. Chapter 22
veeɭakkaarap paʈai Velaikara Battalion Of Velirs
mutalil, pallakkiṉ veɭippuṟattirai - paṉaimarac ciṉṉam uʈaiya tuɳit tirai - vilakiyatu. piṉṉar uɭɭirunta paʈʈut tiraiyum nakarat toʈaŋkiyatu. muṉṉoru taʈavai vallavaraiyaṉ paarttatu pooṉṟa poṉ vaɳɳak kaiyum terintatu. vantiyatteevaṉ iṉi, taaṉ kutirai meeliruppatu takaatu eṉṟu eɳɳi oru noʈiyil kiiɻee kutittaaṉ. First, the outer screens emblazoned with the palmtree symbol parted; next the silken inner curtains were pushed aside. The golden hand seen by Vallavarayan once before, the hand which had stunned him, could be seen once again. Thinking that it was no longer polite to be seated on his horse, Vandiya Devan climbed down.
civikaiyiṉ arukil oʈi vantu, "iɭavaracee! iɭavaracee! pallakkuc cumakkum aaʈkaɭ..." eṉṟu collik koɳʈee aɳɳaantu paarttaaṉ. miiɳʈum uṟṟup paarttaaṉ; kaɳɳimaikaɭai muuʈit tiṟantu meelum paarttaaṉ; paartta kaɳkaɭ kuuciṉa! peeciya naak kuɻaṟiyatu. toɳʈaiyil tiʈiireṉṟu iiram vaṟṟiyatu. "illai, illai! taaŋkaɭ... paɻuvuur iɭavaraci!... paɻuvuur iɭavaraci... uŋkaɭ aaʈkaɭiṉ kutirai eṉ pallakkai iʈittatu!..." eṉṟu uɭaṟik koʈʈiṉaaṉ. He ran up to the palanquin saying "Prince! Prince! Your bearers ..." and looked inside. He looked again; he blinked his eyelids and looked once again! His eyes were dazzled! His tongue tied itself into knots! Suddenly his throat felt parched. "No. No! Princess! Princess of Pazluvoor! ... Your Ladyship, Princess of Pazluvoor! ... Your bearers and their horses dashed against my palanquin ..." came the flustered blabberings.
itellaam kaɳ muuʈit tiṟakkum neerattukkuɭ naʈantatu. pallakkiṉ muṉṉum piṉṉum ceṉṟa veel viirarkaɭ oʈi vantu, vallavaraiyaṉaic cuuɻntu koɳʈaarkaɭ. appaʈi avarkaɭ cuuɻntu koɳʈaarkaɭ eṉpatu vallavaraiyaṉukkum terintatu. avaṉuʈaiya kaiyum iyalpaaka uṟaivaaɭiʈam ceṉṟatu. aaṉaal kaɳkaɭai maʈʈum pallakkiṉ paʈʈut tiraiyiṉ mattiyil oɭirnta mookaṉaaŋkiyiṉ cantira pimpa vataṉattiṉiṉṟum avaṉaal akaṟṟa muʈiyavillai! All this happened within the blink of an eyelid! By now the guards carrying the long spears ran up and surrounded Vandiya Devan. He realized that the men were around him; his hand automatically reached for his sword. But he could not take his eyes off the dazzling face of that enchantress behind the curtains!
aam; vallavaraiyaṉ etirpaarttataṟku maaṟaaka, ippootu appallakkil avaṉ kaɳʈatu oru nijamaaṉa peɳɳiṉ vaʈivantaaṉ! peɳ eṉṟaalum, eppaʈippaʈʈa peɳ! paarttavarkaɭaip paittiyamaaka aʈikkak kuuʈiya ittakaiya peɳɳaɻaku ivvulakil irukkakkuuʈum eṉṟu vantiyatteevaṉ eɳɳiyatee illai! Yes; unlike his expectations, this time he unquestionably saw the beautiful figure of a young girl seated inside the palanquin! Girl means what a girl! Vandiya Devan hadn't known that such beauty, which could madden those who beheld it, could exist in this world.
nalla veeɭaiyaaka, atee nimiʂattil vantiyatteevaṉuʈaiya muuɭai narampu oṉṟu acaintatu. aticayamaaṉa or eɳɳam avaṉ uɭɭattil utayamaayiṟṟu. atai upayookittuk koɭɭat tiirmaaṉittaaṉ. Fortunately, some nerve in his brain became active at that moment. An amazing idea rose in his mind. He decided to make use of that idea.
oru perumuyaṟci ceytu, toɳʈaiyaik kaṉaittu, naaviṟkup peecum caktiyai varavaɻaittuk koɳʈu, "maṉṉikka veeɳʈum! taaŋkaɭ paɻuvuur iɭaiyaraaɳitaaṉee! taŋkaɭaip paarppataṟkaakattaaṉ ittaṉai tuuram vanteeṉ!" eṉṟaaṉ. He made a tremendous effort and cleared his throat, bringing back speech to his tongue. "I beg pardon! My Lady, aren't you the Young-Queen of Pazluvoor? I came this far to make thy acquaintance!" spoke his tongue.
paɻuvuur iɭaiyaraaɳiyiṉ paal vaʈiyum mukattil iɭanakai arumpiyatu. atukaaṟum kuvintirunta taamarai moʈʈu ciṟitu virintu, uɭɭee patittirunta veɳmuttu varicaiyai ileecaakap pulappaʈuttiyatu. antap puṉmuṟuvaliṉ kaanti namatu iɭam viiraṉait tikkumukkaaʈit tiɳaṟac ceytatu. A smile blossomed on the gentle face of the Young-Queen of Pazluvoor. A lotus bud, which was closed shut till that moment, opened slightly to reveal a string of tiny pearls. The radiance of that smile mesmerized our young warrior and absolutely bewildered him.
avaṉarukil vantu niṉṟa viirarkaɭ taŋkaɭ ejamaaṉiyiṉ kaʈʈaɭaikku etirpaarttuk kaattiruntataakat tooṉṟiyatu. antap peɳɳaraci kaiyiṉaal oru camikɲai ceyyavee, avarkaɭ uʈaṉee akaṉṟu pooyc caṟṟut tuurattil vilaki niṉṟaarkaɭ. iraɳʈu viirarkaɭ pallakkiṉ miitu mootik koɳʈu niṉṟa kutiraiyaip piʈittukkoɳʈaarkaɭ. The footmen surrounding him seemed to wait for the commands of their mistress. The Lady made a sign with her finger; they moved away a little and stood apart. Two men caught hold of the horse and waited.
pallakkilirunta peɳɳaraci vantiyatteevaṉai nookkiṉaaɭ. vantiyatteevaṉuʈaiya neɲcil iraɳʈu kuuriya veel muṉaikaɭ paayntaṉa! That jewel among women, seated in the palanquin, looked at Vandiya Devan. Two sharp spear-points pierced his heart!
"aamaam; naaṉ paɻuvuur iɭaiyaraaɳitaaṉ!" eṉṟaaɭ appeɳmaɳi. ivaɭuʈaiya kuralil attakaiya pootai tarum poruɭ eṉṉa kalantirukka muʈiyum? eeṉ ik kuralaik keeʈʈu namatu talai ivvitam kiṟukiṟukka veeɳʈum? "Yes! I am the Young-Queen of Pazluvoor," said that Lady. What is that intoxicating stuff mixed into her voice? Why is my head spinning with such dizziness upon hearing her speak?
"caṟṟu muṉṉaal nii eṉṉa coṉṉaay? eetoo muṟaiyiʈʈaayee? civikai cumakkum aaʈkaɭaip paṟṟi?" "What did you say just now? You appealed to me about something? About my bearers?"
kaacippaʈʈiṉ meṉmaiyum kaɭɭiṉ pootaiyum kaaʈʈut teeṉiṉ iṉippum, kaarkaalattu miṉṉaliṉ jolippum oru peɳ kuralil kalantirukka muʈiyumaa?... avvitam itoo kalantirukkiṉṟaṉavee! Can the softness of Benaras silk, the intoxication of country liquor, the sweetness of forest honey and the flash of monsoon lightning blend like this into the voice of a young maid? It seems quite possible here...
"pallakkaik koɳʈu vantu avarkaɭ uṉ kutiraimiitu mootiṉaarkaɭ eṉṟaa coṉṉaay?"... "You said they dashed my palanquin against your horse?"
paɻuvuur raaɳiyiṉ pavaɭa itaɻkaɭil tavaɻnta parikaacap puṉṉakai, anta veeʈikkaiyai avaɭ naṉku racittataakak kaaʈʈiyatu. itaṉaal vantiyatteevaṉ ciṟitu tuɳiccal aʈaintaaṉ. The mocking smile playing upon her coral red lips indicated that she was enjoying the joke. Vandiya Devan gained some courage because of it.
"aam, makaaraaɳi! ivarkaɭ appaʈittaaṉ ceytaarkaɭ! eṉ kutirai miraɳʈuviʈʈatu!" eṉṟaaṉ. "Yes. Great Queen! These men did that; my horse was petrified."
"niiyum miraɳʈu pooyttaaṉirukkiṟaay! turkkaiyammaṉ kooyil puucaariyiʈam pooy veeppilai aʈikkac collu! payam teɭiyaʈʈum!" "You also look petrified! Go to the priest at the temple of Goddess Durga and ask him to exorcise you! You will overcome this terror!"
itaṟkuɭ vantiyatteevaṉuʈaiya payam naṉku teɭintuviʈʈatu. avaṉukkuc cirippu kuuʈa vantuviʈʈatu. By now, Vandiya Devan had overcome his fear and even laughter came back!
paɻuvuur raaɳiyiṉ mukapaavam ippootu maaṟiviʈʈatu. kuṟunakaiyiṉ nilavu koopak kaṉalaayiṟṟu. The Pazluvoor Queen's expression had now changed: a smiling full-moon turned into blazing anger!
"veeʈikkai appuṟam irukkaʈʈum; uɳmaiyaic col! etaṟkaakap pallakkiṉ meel kutiraiyaik koɳʈu vantu mooti niṟuttiṉaay?" "Stop jesting. Tell the truth. Why did you push your horse against my palanquin and stop me?"
itaṟkut takka maṟumoɻi collittaaṉ aakaveeɳʈum. collaa viʈʈaal...? nallaveeɭaiyaaka, eeṟkaṉavee anta maṟumoɻi vantiyatteevaṉ uɭɭattil utayamaayiruntatu. caṟṟut taɳinta kuralil, piṟar keeʈkakkuuʈaatu eṉṟu veeɳʈumeṉṟee taṉitta antaraŋkam peecum kuralil, "teevi! nantiṉi teevi! aaɻvaarkkaʈiyaaṉ... avaṉ taaṉ, tirumalaiyappaṉ...taŋkaɭaic cantikkumpaʈi coṉṉaaṉ. ataṟkaakavee intac cuuɻcci ceyteeṉ. maṉṉikka veeɳʈum!" eṉṟaaṉ. He had to give a plausible answer. If not... Luckily he had already concocted a story. In a soft voice, a deliberately soft voice, he spoke as if he did not wish others to hear his words: "My Lady! Madam Nandini Devi! He... Mr. Azlvar-adiyan, that is ... Mr. Thirumalai, he ... he asked me to meet you! That is why I connived this strategy. Please forgive me. I beg pardon!"
ivvitam collik koɳʈee paɻuvuur raaɳiyiṉ mukattai vantiyatteevaṉ kuurntu kavaṉittaaṉ. taṉṉuʈaya maṟumoɻiyiṉaal eṉṉa payaṉ viɭaiyap pookiṟatoo eṉṉum aavaluʈaṉ paarttaaṉ. kaṉi marattiṉmeel kal erivatu pooṉṟa kaariyantaaṉ. kaṉi viɻumaa? kaay viɻumaa? eṟinta kal tirumpi viɻumaa? allatu etirpaaraata iʈi eetaavatu viɻumaa? As he said the words he examined her face carefully. He waited anxiously, to see the effect of his reply. It was like throwing a stone at a fruit tree. Will a ripe fruit fall? Or, would it be raw? Or, will the stone fall back on him? Or, will some unexpected thunder descend?
paɻuvuur raaɳiyiṉ kariya puruvaŋkaɭ ciṟitu meelee ceṉṟaṉa. kaɳkaɭil viyappum aiyamum tooṉṟiṉa. maṟukaɳattil antap peɳɳaraci oru muʈivukku vantuviʈʈaaɭ. The dark brows of the Pazluvoor Queen shot up -- surprise and fear filled her intoxicating eyes. The next instant, she seemed to have come to some decision.
"cari; naʈuccaalaiyil niṉṟu peecuvatu ucitam alla; naaɭaikku nam araɳmaṉaikku vaa! ellaa viʂayamum aŋkee viparamaakac collik koɭɭalaam" eṉṟaaɭ. "Fine! It is not safe to stop in the middle of a road and talk. Come to my palace tomorrow. You can explain everything there."
vantiyatteevaṉuʈaiya uɭɭam puurittatu. niṉaitta kaariyam veṟṟi peṟṟuviʈumpoolee kaaɳkiṟatu! aaṉaal mukkaal kiɳaṟu taaɳʈi payaṉillai. maṟṟak kaaṟpaŋkuk kiɳaṟṟaiyum taaɳʈiyaaka veeɳʈum. Vandiya Devan's heart filled with bliss. His intentions were about to be fulfilled, but there was no use in leaping across three fourths of an open well! He must leap across the remaining quarter.
"teevi! teevi! kooʈʈaikkuɭ eṉṉai viʈamaaʈʈaarkaɭee! araɳmaṉaikkuɭɭum viʈamaaʈʈaarkaɭee? eṉṉa ceyvatu?" eṉṟu paraparappuʈaṉ coṉṉaaṉ. "Madam! My Lady! They will not let me come inside the fortress! Neither will they let me enter the palace! What shall I do?" he asked with some agitation.
paɻuvuur raaɳi uʈaṉee pallakkil taṉ arukil kiʈanta oru paʈʈup paiyait tiṟantu ataṟkuɭɭiruntu oru tanta mootirattai mootirattai eʈuttaaɭ. The Young-Queen of Pazluvoor instantly turned aside and picked up a silken purse lying next to her. She opened the bag and took out an ivory signet ring.
"itaik kaaʈʈiṉaal kooʈʈaikkuɭɭum viʈuvaarkaɭ; nam araɳmaṉaikkuɭɭum viʈuvaarkaɭ!" eṉṟu collik koɳʈee koʈuttaaɭ. vantiyatteevaṉ atai aavaluʈaṉ, vaaŋkik koɳʈaaṉ. orukaɳam paṉam ilacciṉai poṟitta anta tanta mootirattaip paarttaaṉ. maṟupaʈi nimirntu raaɳikku vantaṉam kuuṟa eṉɳiyapootu pallakkiṉ tiraikaɭ muuʈik koɳʈiruntaṉa. Saying, "If you show this they will let you into the fort and permit you to enter my palace," she gave him the ring. Vandiya Devan received it eagerly. For a second, he glanced at the ring with the sign of the palm tree. Then, he looked up to thank the lady. But, the screens were pulled shut once again.
aakaa! puuraɳa cantiraṉai raaku kavvumpootu ciṟitu ciṟitaakak kavvukiṟatu. aaṉaal intap pallakkiṉ tiraikaɭ antap peecum nilaa matiyattai oru noʈiyil kapaɭiikaram ceytu viʈʈaṉavee! Ah! An eclipse swallows the full-moon slowly, bit by bit. But these silken screens have covered that eloquent radiance in one moment!
"iṉiyaavatu eṉṉaip piṉ toʈarntu varaatee! apaayam neerum. niṉṟu metuvaaka vaa!" eṉṟu pallakkut tiraikkuɭɭiruntu paʈʈup pooṉṟa kural keeʈʈatu. "Do not follow me anymore. It may be dangerous. Wait and come later," said that silken voice from inside the drapes.
piṟaku pallakku nakarntatu. viirarkaɭ muṉpoolavee ataṉ muṉṉum piṉṉum ceṉṟaarkaɭ. The palanquin moved on. The guards walked ahead, around it like before.
vantiyatteevaṉ kutiraiyiṉ talaik kayiṟṟaip piʈittuk koɳʈu caalaiyooramaaka otuŋki niṉṟaaṉ. paɻuvuur aaɭkaɭil taṉṉai aɳuki vantu peeciyavaṉ iraɳʈu muuṉṟu taʈavai tirumpit tirumpip paarttatai avaṉuʈaiya kaɳkaɭ kavaṉittu uɭmaṉattukkuc ceyti aṉuppiṉa. Vandiya Devan stood by the roadside, holding the reins of his horse. His eyes noted that the Pazluvoor footman who had stopped to talk to him looked back several times; the message reached his inner brain.
aam; avaṉuʈaiya veɭi maṉam pallakkilirunta paɻuvuur raaɳiyiṉ mookaṉa vaʈivattaic cuṟṟic cuṟṟi vantukoɳʈiruntatu. ittaṉai neeram kaɳʈatu, keeʈʈatu ellaam uɳmai taaṉaa? allatu oru maaya maṉookarak kaṉavaa? ippaʈiyum oru aɻaki, oru cauntariya vaʈivam, intap puuvulakil kaaɳa muʈiyumaa! His outer brain revolved around the enchanting face of the Young-Queen of Pazluvoor. Did all this truly happen? Was everything a pleasing dream? Can there be such beauty, such a ravishing form on this earth?
arampai, uurvaci, meeṉakai eṉṟellaam teevamaatarkaɭ iruppataakap puraaɳaŋkaɭil colvatuɳʈu. avarkaɭuʈaiya aɻaku, muṟṟum tuṟanta muṉivarkaɭiṉ tavattaiyum paŋkam ceytataakak keeʈʈatuɳʈu. aaṉaal inta ulakattil... periya paɻuveeʈʈaraiyar inta mookiṉiyiṉ kaalaʈiyil aʈimai puuɳʈu kiʈappataaka naaʈu nakaraŋkaɭil peecuvatellaam uɳmaiyaakavee irukkalaam. iruntaal, atil viyappu oṉṟum iraatu! narai tirai muuppuk kaɳʈavarum, teekamellaam poork kaayaŋkaɭuʈaṉ kaʈuuramaaṉa tooṟṟaŋ koɳʈavarumaaṉa paɻuveeʈʈaraiyar eŋkee? sukumaariyum kaʈʈaɻakiyumaaṉa inta iɭam maŋkai eŋkee? ivaɭuʈaiya oru puṉṉakaiyaip peṟuvataṟkaaka antak kiɻavar eṉṉa kaariyantaaṉ ceyya maaʈʈaar?... Myth and fable talk of divine maidens -- heavenly beauties called Ramba, Urvasi and Menaka. There are tales about how those beautiful maidens disturbed the penance of ascetics who foreswore all worldly pleasures. But in reality! ... There must be truth in the rumor about the Elder Lord Pazluvoor being a bonded slave to this ravishing lady. There is no surprise if it is true. What contrast between Lord Pazluvoor -- old and grey, with a body disfigured by scars of several battle wounds -- and this soft, gentle, dazzling maiden? What deeds will that old man perform, to obtain one smile from her?
veku neeram caalai orattil niṉṟu ivvitam cintaṉaikaɭil aaɻntirunta piṟaku vantiyatteevaṉ kutiraimeel eeṟik koɳʈu meɭɭa meɭɭa atait taɲcaik kooʈʈaiyai nookkic celuttiṉaaṉ. He waited for a long time by the roadside, mulling over such thoughts. Finally, he mounted his horse and slowly rode towards the gates of Tanjore.
cuuriyaṉ astamikkum neerattil pirataaṉa kooʈʈai vaacalai aʈaintaaṉ. kooʈʈaikkuc caṟṟut tuurattileeyee nakaram aarampamaakiyiruntatu. vitavitamaaṉa paɳʈaŋkaɭ viṟkum kaʈai viitikaɭum, palavakait toɻilkaɭil iiʈupaʈʈa makkaɭ vaaɻum terukkaɭum, kooʈʈaiyaic cuṟṟi aʈukkaʈukkaaka amaintiruntaṉa. viitikaɭil poovoorum varuvoorum paɳʈaŋkaɭ vaaŋkuvoorum vilai kuuṟuvoorum maaʈu puuʈʈiya vaɳʈikaɭum kutirai puuʈʈiya rataŋkaɭum niṟaintu, eŋkum oree kalakalappaayiruntatu. anta viitikaɭukkuɭɭee pukuntu ceṉṟu cooɻa naaʈʈup putiya talainakarattil vaaɻum makkaɭaiyum, avarkaɭ vaaɻum vitattaiyum paarkka vantiyatteevaṉukku mikka aavalaayiruntatu. aaṉaal ataṟkellaam ippootu avakaacam illai. vanta kaariyattai mutalil paarkka veeɳʈum. veeʈikkai paarppatellaam piṟpaaʈu vaittukkoɭɭa veeɳʈum. By sunset, he neared the main entrance to the city fort. The city extended for some distance before the fortress walls and gates. Market streets offering several kinds of goods for sale, residential streets of people engaged in various trades -- these successively surrounded the outer boundaries of the fort. All the streets were busy with the hustle and bustle of a large town: people going hither and thither, merchants haggling over the price of goods they sold, carts and wagons drawn by hefty, well fed bullocks, horse drawn chariots, palanquins and litters filled the streets. Vandiya Devan was eager to enter those streets and experience the sights and sounds of the new capital of the Chozlas; he wished to make the acquaintance of these city folk and become familiar with their ways. But, there was no time for all that now. He must first complete the assignment on which he had journeyed so far. Sightseeing must wait.
inta tiirmaaṉattuʈaṉ vantiyatteevaṉ taɲcai nakariṉ pirataaṉa vaacalai aɳukiṉaaṉ. kooʈʈai vaacalil pirammaaɳʈamaaṉa katavukaɭ accamayam caattiyiruntaṉa. vaacalil niṉṟa kaavalarkaɭ makkaɭai otuŋkac ceytu viiti oraŋkaɭil niṟkumpaʈi ceytu koɳʈiruntaarkaɭ. makkaɭum otuŋki niṉṟaarkaɭ. aam avaravarkaɭ taŋkaɭ aluvalkaɭaip paarttukkoɳʈu poovataṟkup patilaaka, eetoo uurvalam allatu pavaṉi paarppataṟkaakak kaattiruppavarkaɭaip pool niṉṟaarkaɭ. aaɳkaɭ, peɳkaɭ, kuɻantaikaɭ, vayootikar ellaarumee aavaluʈaṉ niṉṟaarkaɭ. With this determination he neared the main gates of Tanjore. The massive doors of the main gateway were closed shut at that time. The guards and gatekeepers outside were trying to maneuver the people making them stand in an orderly fashion on both sides of the street. The people complied and stood aside. Yes; instead of going about their own business they stood by the roadside as if in anticipation of some procession or parade. Men, Women, children and elderly -- everybody waited.
kooʈʈai vaacalukku muṉṉaal ciṟitu tuuram varai veṟumaiyaakavee iruntatu. vaacalaɳʈai kaavalarkaɭ maʈʈum niṉṟaarkaɭ. For a short distance the street in front of the gateway was empty. The guards stood near the doorway.
viʂayam eṉṉaveṉṟu terintukoɭɭa vantiyatteevaṉ aaval koɳʈaaṉ. ellaarum otuŋki niṟkumpootu taaṉ maʈʈum kooʈʈai vaacal kaappaaɭariʈam ceṉṟu muʈʈik koɭɭa avaṉ virumpavillai. atiliruntu viiɳ vaatamum caɳʈaiyum muuɭalaam. ippootu taṉakkuk kaariyam mukkiyamee tavira viiriyam peritu alla. viiɳ caɳʈaikaɭil iṟaŋka itu taruɳamalla. Vandiya Devan wished to find out what was happening. He did not wish to entangle himself with the gatekeepers when everyone else stood aside. Unnecessary dispute and trouble might come of it. The job on hand was more important than a joust. It was not the time to pick an unwanted quarrel.
eṉavee, vantiyatteevaṉ kooʈʈai vaacalaik kavaṉikkak kuuʈiya iʈattil viiti orattil otuŋki niṉṟaaṉ. pakkattil kammeṉṟu malariṉ maɳam viiciyatu. tirumpip paarttaaṉ. oru vaalipaṉ, tiruniiṟu ruttiraaʈcam mutaliya civac ciṉṉaŋkaɭ tarittavaṉ, iraɳʈu kaikaɭilum iraɳʈu puukkuuʈaikaɭuʈaṉ niṟpataik kaɳʈaaṉ. Therefore, he stood to one side at a spot where he could keep an eye on the gateway. The heady fragrance of flowers rose by his side. He looked around. A youth, wearing the symbols of a Saiva devotee, such as rudraksha beads around his neck and ashen marks on his forehead, stood by his side carrying two large baskets of flowers in both his hands.

Vandiya Devan had heard much about the brave warriors of the Velaikara Battalion. It was an important organization in the ancient Tamil nations, particularly in Chozla dominions. `Velaikaras' were the personal bodyguards of the ruling monarch. But there was an important difference between them and ordinary bodyguards. The men of this battalion had taken individual oaths to "Personally guard the life and person of the king, giving up their own life if the need arises." If something untoward happened to the king or his life, because of their carelessness or in spite of their guard, they had sworn to cut off their heads with their own sword and offer themselves as a sacrifice to the Goddess Durga. It was natural that such men, who had sworn such terrible oaths, were given ceratin privileges.

The two large doors of the gateway opened with a loud noise. Two horsemen emerged first. In their right hands they held aloft two narrow pennants flying high. They were curious in design. The red cloth of the banner was painted with a leaping tiger and a shining crown below it; underneath the crown was a sacrificial alter with a severed head on it and a huge sacrificial sword lay next to it. The pennant was quite terrifying to behold.
